When Work Goes Wrong: Why AV Professionals Matter

Christina Tollefson September 4, 2025 3 min read

We've all seen it happen — someone says they can take on a project, but the results show they're in over their head. In the world of audio, video, and home automation, "close enough" can quickly become a costly headache.

A Quick Story About a "NOT"

Sometimes you have a project, need a solution or repair, and someone tells you they can do it. Sometimes they bite off more than they can chew. Some people don't stay in their lane. Sometimes it works out fine. Sometimes not. This is a quick story about a "NOT."

The Original Installation

We configured a home nine years ago with a dedicated home theater complete with LED indirect lighting on some soffits and edges, fiber-optic starlight ceiling, 7.2 surround system, complete home automation, multiple touchpanels, and speakers throughout — inside and out.

Water Damage and Stucco Repairs

The home suffered water damage due to a roof leak. After the roof was repaired, the homeowner called a stucco company to repair an outdoor ceiling area. This area has lights and a pair of Monitor Audio speakers in the cement/stucco ceiling.

I stopped by to check on the repair status at one point and discovered the stucco company had totally covered the area where the two large round speakers were supposed to be reinstalled. I asked about the holes; they stated, "if you provide the circle dimension, we will cut the holes."

The "Cement Beaver" Holes

I returned with the speakers (which need a nice round hole of a very specific size) for the install — and was shocked. I had made the incorrect assumption that the company doing the stucco work was well-versed and capable of easily creating a professional, concentric hole far better than I, the "A/V guy."

It appears they brought their favorite "Concrete Beaver" along to gnaw a hole in the stucco ceiling. They were also aware the holes were for 4"-deep speakers — and they made the hole directly under a truss, which could not be altered.

Taking Matters Into My Own Hands

I called the contractor and he told me to do what I needed to make it work — they'd handle the repairs. I grabbed my trusty Dewalt reciprocating saw, installed my carbide blade (perfect for cutting neatly through cement and stucco), traced a new hole (relocated slightly to avoid the truss hidden above), and proceeded to cut the holes I previously and incorrectly assumed they were going to make.

The Real Moral of the Story

Sometimes a "handyman quality" person is capable of performing tasks or completing repairs. Sometimes you need to call a professional. This is one of those latter times. The portion that really floors me is that I was able to perform the part of the repair that should be in their wheelhouse. I should be calling them to fix my screw-up in the stucco — not the A/V guy doing a better job than them.

The project should have been completed a month ago, but it required three more trips because they bit off more than they could chew.

The bitterness of poor quality remains long after the sweetness of low price is forgotten. — Benjamin Franklin

"Just because you CAN doesn't mean you SHOULD." Unfortunately, this still rings true today.

If It Matters, Call a Professional

If in doubt, call a professional. If it's important that a good job is done, call a professional. If you CARE, call a professional. Or at least someone that cares.
