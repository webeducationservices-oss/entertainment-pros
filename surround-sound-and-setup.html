Surround Sound

Surround Sound and the Art of Setup: Where Tech Meets Configuration

Chip Tollefson February 5, 2026 5 min read

You can buy great speakers and still end up with disappointing sound.

We see it all the time. A homeowner invests in a "nice" sound system, turns it on, and the result feels like louder TV sound — not a true movie or music experience. Dialogue is hard to understand. Bass is boomy. Surround effects feel random. Or the system just sounds like money was wasted.

The issue usually isn't the brand or the price tag. Sound quality comes from two things working together:

  • The technology you choose (speaker type, receiver/amp, subwoofer, processing)
  • The configuration you build (speaker placement, angles, distances, calibration, and room design)

At Entertainment Pros, we treat surround sound like a system, not a box of gear. The goal is not just to install equipment — the goal is to design a sound field that fits your room, your seating, and what you actually love to watch and listen to.

What "Surround" Really Means

Surround sound is not just having more speakers. True surround sound means the speakers sit outside the seating area so the listener is enclosed by audio. The goal is to create an immersive sound field where audio can come from the front, sides, and behind you in a way that feels natural.

If all your speakers are in front of you, you can still get better sound than a TV. But you will not get the "you are inside the scene" experience that makes surround sound special.

  • The front speakers create the main soundstage: dialogue, music, and the bulk of the action.
  • Surround speakers fill the space on the sides and behind you.
  • A subwoofer adds depth and impact so sound feels physical, not thin.

Home Theater Design: What We Do First

Great surround sound starts long before installation day. We begin with room-first design — meaning we design the system around your space, not the other way around.

Step 1: We learn how you actually use the room

The best system for a movie lover is not always the best system for someone who mainly listens to music. A living-room install has different constraints than a dedicated theater room. We ask:

  • Movies, sports, gaming, or music — what matters most?
  • Where is the primary seating position?
  • How many seats need "great sound" vs "good sound"?
  • Bookshelf, in-wall, or in-ceiling speakers?
  • Maximum performance, clean aesthetics, or a balance?

Step 2: We evaluate the room layout and surfaces

Hard floors, big windows, and open floor plans can create reflections (echoes) that affect clarity. A sectional against the wall changes the surround placement options. A vaulted ceiling impacts overhead sound. We look at room dimensions, ceiling height, seating distance from the screen, reflective surfaces, and where wiring can be hidden cleanly.

Step 3: We choose the right equipment for the room, not just the budget

Two people can buy the same speakers and get completely different results based on room and setup. Speaker dispersion, power needs, subwoofer output, receiver capabilities, and calibration tools all factor in.

Speaker Angles Matter: Real-World Best Practices

If the angles are right, sound feels cohesive, spacious, and intentional. If the angles are off, even expensive gear can sound unbalanced.

Front left and right speakers: 45 degrees

A solid starting point for many rooms is placing the front left and right speakers at roughly 45 degrees relative to the main listening position. This creates a wide soundstage without losing clarity. Voices and key sound effects feel anchored to the screen, music and movies sound wide, and you can locate instruments and effects without strain.

Surround speakers: 60 to 90 degrees

For surround speakers, the goal is immersion. We often use in-ceiling speakers rather than bookshelf speakers mounted to a wall — many rooms don't have 10' behind the seating location for proper alignment. In-ceiling surrounds let one speaker sit 7' from a viewer while the other is 10' away, much closer to equal than wall-mounted alternatives.

Surround sound should feel like it surrounds you — not like speakers are shouting at your ears.

Configuration and Calibration: The Part That Makes It All Click

Configuration is how we tell the system what's connected and how the sound should behave. Calibration is how we make it accurate in your space. All the receivers we sell ship with a calibration microphone, but that's only the beginning. We listen to a few different songs, ask what kind of music you listen to, and tune it for you. This makes a tremendous difference over relying on the auto-setup mic alone.

What we configure in a typical surround install:

  • Speaker distances so timing aligns correctly
  • Speaker levels so channels match in volume
  • Crossover settings so bass is clean and speakers only play their proper frequencies
  • Subwoofer integration so low-end feels powerful but not muddy
  • Room correction to reduce problems caused by reflections and room shape

Why calibration matters for dialogue

Most surround complaints are about dialogue, not effects. The fix is usually center-channel positioning and aiming, correct levels and tonal balance, softening reflections with sound-absorptive surfaces, and keeping bass from masking voices.

Aesthetics vs Audio: What Matters More?

Some people want maximum performance. Some want the cleanest look possible. Most want a smart balance. Options to protect your room's look without killing sound:

  • In-wall speakers for a clean, built-in finish
  • In-ceiling speakers for surrounds or overhead channels
  • On-wall speakers — sleek and great-sounding
  • Hidden wiring and cable management
  • Subwoofer placement planning so it doesn't dominate the design

Why Soundbars Are Better Than TV Sound, But Not Surround Sound

Soundbars solve a real problem — TV speakers fire toward the back wall and bounce their way to your ears. A soundbar gives you better clarity firing directly at you, a wider soundstage than the TV alone, and often improved bass with a subwoofer bundle.

But a soundbar is still mostly a front-of-room experience. The speakers are not wider than your listening area at the angles required to sound like the scene happens around you. Some soundbars simulate surround using processing — they're tricking your ears. True surround uses discrete speakers placed around the seating area, creating real directionality and movement.

If you want surround effects moving around you, gaming audio that helps you locate direction, or a theater-like experience, a soundbar is a stepping stone — not the destination.
